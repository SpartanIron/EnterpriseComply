How to Read This Plan
This plan is organized into five sequential phases spanning approximately 18–24 months. Each phase builds on the last. Phases 1–2 make the platform safe enough for client use. Phases 3–4 achieve compliance readiness. Phase 5 achieves formal authorization. Within each phase, tasks are organized by workstream and include the specific location within EnterpriseComply where the work is done, the framework control(s) it satisfies, and the expected output/artifact.
Effort notation: S = Small (hours), M = Medium (days), L = Large (weeks), XL = Large (months)

PHASE 0: EMERGENCY ACTIONS — Complete Within 72 Hours
These are non-negotiable security minimums that must be done before any client uploads any document to the platform.

0.1 — Enable MFA on the Administrator Account
Where in the app: Settings → Security tab → Authenticator App (2FA) → "Set up"
What to do: Install Google Authenticator, Authy, or 1Password on the administrator's mobile device. Click "Set up," scan the QR code, enter the 6-digit TOTP code to confirm, and save. Verify that "Two-factor auth" in the Account section changes from "Disabled" to "Enabled."
Why this is an emergency: The admin account currently authenticates via magic link only — meaning anyone who compromises the registered email (annankwekujude@gmail.com) gains full super-admin access to all client data, all evidence, all integrations, and all uploaded documents. This is a single point of total failure. Furthermore, MFA is required by CMMC IA.3.083, FedRAMP IA-2(1), and SOC 2 CC6.1 — failing this check stops all three framework assessments cold.
Frameworks satisfied: CMMC IA.3.083, FedRAMP IA-2, SOC 2 CC6.1
Effort: S — 15 minutes
Artifact: Screenshot of enabled 2FA status in Settings → Security

0.2 — Move Admin Account to a Corporate Email Domain
Where in the app: Settings → Security → Account section
What to do: Change the administrator email from the personal Gmail address to a corporate domain address (e.g., kweku@colorcodesolutions.com). Never use personal email accounts for production system administration. Personal email accounts are not subject to organizational security controls (e.g., corporate MFA policies, session management, conditional access).
Why this is an emergency: The current admin email is a personal Gmail account. If that Gmail account is compromised via phishing or credential stuffing, the attacker has full access to the entire platform with zero organizational ability to revoke access. Corporate email allows the organization to enforce conditional access, revoke credentials centrally, and apply DLP controls.
Frameworks satisfied: CMMC IA.3.058 (Identify and authenticate organizational users), FedRAMP AC-2, SOC 2 CC6.2
Effort: S — 30 minutes
Artifact: Updated account email in Settings

0.3 — Create a Minimum Second Admin Account
Where in the app: Settings → Users & Roles → Invite user
What to do: Create at least one additional admin-level account assigned to a different person (or a break-glass account with a separate corporate email), also with MFA enabled. This ensures no single person's absence or account compromise locks the organization out or creates a single point of failure.
Frameworks satisfied: CMMC AC.2.006 (Limit use of portable storage), CMMC IA.3.083, SOC 2 CC6.2, FedRAMP AC-2
Effort: S — 30 minutes
Artifact: User roster showing minimum two accounts with MFA-enabled status

WORKSTREAM 1B: Policy Publication
The platform has 9 policy drafts. None are published, none have been reviewed, and none have been formally distributed to staff. All of them must be reviewed, finalized, and published before any compliance assessment can proceed.
Step 1.5 — Review and Publish All 9 Draft Policies
Where in the app: Policies section → Open each policy → review content → click Publish
For each policy, the review process is: Open the policy, read the full text, edit any sections that do not reflect current actual practice (do not publish aspirational policies — publish what you actually do or commit to do), assign a Policy Owner (a named individual), set a review cycle (annual for most, semi-annual for MFA and incident response), obtain management approval (documented via signature or email confirmation), and then publish.
The nine policies and their priority order are:
First, Multi-Factor Authentication Policy — highest urgency given MFA is currently disabled. This policy must reflect that MFA is required for all accounts, TOTP is the approved method, and exceptions require written CISO approval. Controls: UCO-AI-001. Framework: CMMC IA.3.083, FedRAMP IA-2.
Second, Privileged Access Management Policy — governs who can have admin access, how access is provisioned and reviewed, and what happens when someone leaves. Controls: UCO-AC-002, UCO-AI-002. Framework: CMMC AC.2.006, FedRAMP AC-6(5).
Third, Data Breach Response Policy — must specify: what constitutes a breach, who must be notified and in what timeframe (72 hours for GDPR, as soon as practicable under federal requirements), who leads response, and how post-incident reviews are conducted. Controls: UCO-IR-001, UCO-IR-003. Framework: All three frameworks have incident response requirements.
Fourth, Secure Communications Policy — governs encryption requirements for data in transit, approved communication tools, and prohibitions on sending sensitive data via unencrypted channels. Controls: UCO-DP-003. Framework: CMMC SC.3.177, FedRAMP SC-8.
Fifth, Endpoint Security Policy — governs EDR requirements, full disk encryption, MDM enrollment, and removable media controls. Controls: UCO-EP-001 through UCO-EP-004. Framework: CMMC MP.2.120, FedRAMP SC-28.
Sixth, Risk Assessment Policy — governs how risk assessments are conducted, how frequently, who conducts them, and how the risk register is maintained. Controls: UCO-RM-001. Framework: CMMC CA.2.157, FedRAMP RA-3.
Seventh, CMMC Compliance Policy — the overarching commitment to maintaining CMMC Level 2 compliance, including scope, CUI handling requirements, subcontractor requirements, and the annual self-assessment or C3PAO assessment schedule.
Eighth, FedRAMP Compliance Policy — the commitment to FedRAMP authorization pursuit, including the authorization boundary, inherited controls from the cloud service provider, and the continuous monitoring obligations.
Ninth, delete the "test" draft — it is a test artifact that should not appear in the production policy library.
Effort per policy: M (2–3 days each for review, edit, approval). Total: L — 3–4 weeks
Artifact: All 8 policies showing Published status with review dates and policy owner names

WORKSTREAM 2A: Infrastructure Decisions
Step 2.1 — Formalize Cloud Infrastructure Architecture Decision
What to do: Make an explicit, documented decision about the hosting infrastructure for EnterpriseComply. This is the single most consequential architectural decision for the path to FedRAMP. The options are:
Option A (Recommended for FedRAMP path): Migrate to a FedRAMP-authorized cloud service provider — AWS GovCloud (East/West), Azure Government, or Google Cloud for Government. This is required for FedRAMP Moderate authorization and significantly simplifies the control inheritance story. Estimated migration effort: 2–4 months.
Option B (SOC 2 / CMMC only): Remain on commercial cloud (e.g., AWS us-east-1 or similar) with full implementation of all controls without FedRAMP CSP inheritance. This is viable for SOC 2 Type II and CMMC Level 2 but will not satisfy FedRAMP Moderate.
Document the decision in an Architecture Decision Record (ADR), including the rationale, the selected CSP, the geographic region (US-only required for federal data), and the planned migration timeline.
Frameworks satisfied: FedRAMP SA-9, SC-7; CMMC SC.3.187; SOC 2 A1.1
